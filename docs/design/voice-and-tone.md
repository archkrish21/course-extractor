# Plan with Genie — Voice & Tone

> **Audience:** anyone writing user-facing copy — marketing pages, in-app microcopy, onboarding, emails, errors, empty states.
>
> **Companion doc:** [`brand.md`](./brand.md) defines the visual system (mascot, logo, wordmark, palette). This doc defines how Genie *sounds*.

---

## TL;DR — the three rules

1. **Genie is a wise expert, not a miracle vendor.** The magic is in the knowledge, not in passivity.
2. **Clarity wins on decision surfaces. Warmth wins everywhere else.**
3. **"Wish" language belongs in microcopy and ambient moments — not on primary CTAs, pricing, or anything a buyer is comparing.**

If you remember nothing else, remember these three.

---

## Who Genie is

Genie is the expert friend every student wishes they had: an older sibling who already navigated high school, a counselor who somehow has time, an advisor who's always calm. Genie has read every course catalog and knows exactly which path leads where.

**Genie is:**
- **Confident but never condescending** — Genie knows the system cold, but never talks down to a 14-year-old figuring out their first schedule.
- **Warm, not syrupy** — real warmth, not cloying cheerfulness. Genie doesn't use exclamation points like a cereal mascot.
- **Playful in small moments, serious when it counts** — a wink in a toast message, plain language in a graduation-requirement warning.
- **An advisor, not a magician** — Genie makes things *clearer*, not *easier* in a way that bypasses the work.

**Genie is not:**
- Silly, slapstick, or immature.
- Breathless, hypey, or "disruptive."
- Stuffy, corporate, or lawyerly.
- A fortune-teller. Genie doesn't predict; Genie plans.

## The wish-language rule

"Wish" is Genie's signature metaphor. It's also the place it's easiest to overuse. Treat it like saffron — a pinch transforms the dish; a tablespoon ruins it.

### Where wish language works

| Surface | Example | Why it works |
|---|---|---|
| Empty states | *"No plan yet. What shall we make of your four years?"* | Reader is engaged; warmth converts hesitation to action |
| Success toasts | *"Wish locked in."* | Delight moment; reinforces the brand without impeding flow |
| Onboarding | *"Three questions. Then the magic starts."* | Low-stakes, sets the voice |
| Long-loading states | *"Consulting the stars…"* | Time to notice; personality earns the wait |
| 404 / empty error pages | *"Even genies lose track sometimes."* | Softens a bad moment that isn't high-stakes |
| Email subject lines | *"Your plan, one step closer."* | Competes for inbox attention |

### Where wish language fails

| Surface | Bad | Better | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| Primary CTA buttons | *"Grant my wish for free"* | *"Get Started Free"* | Decision moment. Clarity converts. |
| Hero headline | *"Make your 4-year HS wish!"* | *"Your four-year plan. Granted."* | Must explain product in <2s. A nod is enough; a lecture loses them. |
| Pricing tiers | *"The Three-Wish Plan"* | *"Plus"* / *"Elite"* | Buyers comparing prices need clarity, not theme. |
| Errors affecting data | *"The magic fizzled"* | *"Couldn't save your changes. Please try again."* | Real errors deserve real language. |
| Legal, privacy, billing | — | Plain, direct | Trust moments. No whimsy. |

### The test

Before using *wish / grant / magic* in a string, ask: **is the user making a decision right now?** If yes, cut it. If no, consider it — but still earn it. One wish-beat per page, at most.

## "I" vs "we" — who's speaking

Genie is a character, but the product is made by a team. The copy has to acknowledge both — without confusing them.

| Speaker | Use when | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **"I"** — Genie as a character | In-app moments where Genie is the advisor performing an action for the student | *"Pick the courses you want. I'll flag the prereqs."* / *"I've mapped your AP track — want to tweak it?"* |
| **"we"** — the team / company | Errors, legal, billing, support, and marketing-body copy where the company is explicitly speaking | *"We couldn't save your changes."* / *"We're expanding to more schools soon."* |

### The rule

- **Default to "I"** in the product UI — onboarding, empty states, success toasts, inline guidance, gentle corrections. This is where the mascot earns its keep.
- **Switch to "we"** when the team — not the character — is responsible. Especially anything involving money, data loss, privacy, or a promise the company is making.
- **Never mix voices inside a single surface.** If the hero says *"we'll help,"* the subhead shouldn't say *"I'll map."*
- **Never anthropomorphize data-loss errors.** *"I couldn't save"* misattributes agency to a mascot who can't fix the server. The team owns that failure — *"we couldn't save."*

### A note on inclusive "we"

Phrases like *"Let's plan your senior year"* use an implicit inclusive "we" (Genie + the student, together). This is warm and welcome in onboarding or collaborative moments. Just don't write *"We'll plan"* when what you mean is Genie alone — use *"I'll plan"* for that, and reserve "we" for moments that are truly collaborative or truly team-voice.

### Why it matters

The brand spent real effort ([`brand.md`](./brand.md)) making Genie a *character* — green skin, turban, hand-tuned wordmark. Speaking as "we" everywhere wastes that equity and flattens the product to generic SaaS voice. The mascot's presence in copy is "I."

## Voice principles

### 1. Short sentences. Real verbs.

**Do:** *"Plan your path. Track your grades. Share with family."*
**Don't:** *"Our comprehensive platform empowers students to holistically manage their academic journey."*

If you're reaching for a thesaurus, you're writing for the wrong audience.

### 2. Address the student as "you."

**Do:** *"Pick the courses you want. I'll flag the prereqs."*
**Don't:** *"Students can select desired courses while the platform validates prerequisite chains."*

Write to the reader as "you" — never "users," "students," or "the user." (For whether Genie speaks back as *I* or *we*, see [above](#i-vs-we--whos-speaking).)

### 3. Explain the *why*, not just the *what*.

**Do:** *"One wrong 9th-grade choice can block AP Calc in 12th. Plan all four years now so that doesn't happen."*
**Don't:** *"Prerequisite validation."*

Students don't buy features. They buy outcomes and reassurance.

### 4. Earn every exclamation point.

Default to periods. An exclamation point should feel like Genie genuinely leaned in. Most of the time it doesn't.

### 5. Don't sell with fear.

*"Don't mess up high school"* is out. *"Plan with confidence"* is in. Parents already worry enough; the brand should lower the temperature, not raise it.

## Tone by context

Voice is constant. Tone flexes. Genie sounds different when you've just mapped out a four-year AP track than when the app just lost your draft.

| Context | Tone | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Neutral task** | Clear, warm, brief | *"Course added to fall semester."* |
| **Celebration** | Playful, generous | *"Four years mapped. Nice work."* |
| **Gentle correction** | Helpful, non-judgmental | *"Heads up — Algebra 2 needs to come before Pre-Calc."* |
| **Hard error (data-loss risk)** | Plain, direct, apologetic | *"We couldn't save your changes. Please try again."* No metaphors. |
| **Empty state** | Inviting, optional wish-voice | *"Nothing here yet. Let's fix that."* |
| **Trust moment (billing, privacy, legal)** | Formal, specific, zero whimsy | *"Your card will be charged $9.99 on the 15th of each month."* |

## Surface-by-surface guide

| Surface | Voice dial | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Primary CTAs | **Functional** — proven patterns | "Get Started Free", "Start Free Trial", "Save Plan" |
| Secondary CTAs | Light brand voice OK | "See how it works", "Make a wish" (linked to demo) |
| Hero headline | Value-first with a brand touch | "Your four-year plan. Granted." |
| Hero subheadline | Warm, specific, benefit-driven | "Tell Genie what you want from four years — AP tracks, a balanced load, a clean path to graduation. We'll help you map it." |
| Section headers | Clear with occasional lift | "What Genie solves" / "Three steps. No incantations." |
| Feature descriptions | Benefit first, mechanism second | "Never get blocked from a course again. Genie flags prereq conflicts before you commit." |
| Form labels | Plain | "Email", "Password" — not "Summon your credentials" |
| Placeholder text | Clear, occasionally warm | "e.g., AP Calc BC" — not "e.g., wish for one…" |
| Buttons | Verb + object, concise | "Add course", "Save plan" — not "Enact the plan" |
| Error toasts | Plain, actionable | "Couldn't load courses. Check your connection." |
| Success toasts | Warm, brief, occasional wish-beat | "Plan saved." / "Wish locked in." |
| Loading (short, <500ms) | Silent or "Loading…" | No theming |
| Loading (long) | Personality earns the wait | "Consulting the stars…" |
| Onboarding | Full brand voice, Genie speaks as "I" | "Three questions. Then I'll get you started." |
| Empty states | Inviting, wish-voice OK | "No courses yet. What shall we plan?" |
| Email subject | Warm, specific, clickable | "Your plan, one step closer" |
| Email body | Warm but substantive | Human paragraphs, no stacked emoji |
| Marketing social | Playful, mascot-forward | Full brand voice |
| Legal / privacy / billing | Plain, formal, specific | No voice. Say exactly what happens. |
| Alt text, ARIA labels | Descriptive, no theming | "Icon: course planner grid" |

## Vocabulary

### Preferred

- **Plan** (not "schedule" or "curriculum")
- **Path** (not "track" — clearer for parents)
- **Four years** (not "4-year" — reads more naturally in prose)
- **Course** (not "class" — matches catalog language)
- **Granted** (confident, one-word brand moment)
- **Map / mapped** (great verb — concrete, visual, shared with the product)
- **On course** (the double meaning is the point)

### Use sparingly — one per page, at most

- **Wish**
- **Magic**
- **Grant**
- **Spell**

### Avoid

- **Journey** — fine in small doses, but overused across ed-tech. Every competitor has a "journey."
- **Dream** — too vague; "wish" carries more brand weight, so pick one and commit.
- **Unleash / Unlock** — SaaS cliché.
- **Revolutionize / Empower / Transform** — any marketing verb a Series-B deck would use.
- **Your success** — empty phrase.
- **Disrupt** — not for students, not for parents.

### Never

- **"Your wish is my command"** — the one Aladdin phrase that collapses all our brand-differentiation work.
- **"Rub the lamp" / "Three wishes"** — same.
- **"Poof!"** — immature.
- **Performative Gen Z slang** — students can smell it.

## Writing patterns

### Rhythm

Vary sentence length. Three short sentences in a row feel punchy. Three long sentences in a row feel corporate. Mix.

### Openings

Start with the reader's situation, not with Genie.

**Do:** *"You've got 300 courses to choose from."*
**Don't:** *"Genie helps you choose courses."*

### Closings

Don't editorialize at the end of a paragraph. Trust the reader to draw the conclusion.

### Punctuation

- Em dash for asides, over parentheses.
- Oxford comma, always.
- No double spaces.
- Ellipses only for true trailing-off — not for suspense.

### Capitalization

- **Sentence case** in buttons, headers, and nav. Not Title Case. ("Save plan", not "Save Plan")
- Exception: proper nouns and product tier names — **Plus**, **Elite**, **Starter**.

## The copy review checklist

Before shipping user-facing copy, read it back and ask:

- [ ] Could a 14-year-old read this without pausing?
- [ ] Would a parent trust me after reading this?
- [ ] Did I use *wish / magic / grant* more than once on this surface?
- [ ] Is the primary CTA a plain, proven verb phrase?
- [ ] Did I lean on SaaS cliché verbs (*unlock, empower, transform*)?
- [ ] If this is an error, does it tell the user what to do next?
- [ ] If this is a trust moment (billing, privacy, legal), did I strip all whimsy?
- [ ] Could I cut 20% of the words without losing meaning?

## Examples — before and after

### Hero headline

**Before:** *Plan Your 4-Year High School Journey*
**After:** *Your four-year plan. Granted.*

Keeps scannability. Adds one brand beat. "Granted" reads as a confident promise, not a passive gift.

### Hero subheadline

**Before:** *Track courses, grades, and GPA. Monitor graduation requirements. Keep parents and counselors in the loop — all in one place.*
**After:** *Tell Genie what you want from four years — AP tracks, a balanced load, a clean path to graduation. We'll help you map it, share it with family, and keep it on course.*

Same information. Adds Genie voice. Makes the benefit explicit instead of listing features.

### Primary CTA

**Before:** *Get Started Free*
**After:** *Get Started Free* (unchanged — don't fix what converts)

### Secondary CTA

**Before:** *See How It Works*
**After:** *Make a wish* (links to interactive demo)

One earned brand beat at a low-stakes entry point.

### Final CTA section header

**Before:** *Start planning your future today*
**After:** *Make the wish. Map the path.*

Two beats — aspirational and concrete. Earns "wish" because the surrounding content has already done the explaining.

### Empty planner state

**Before:** *No courses added to this semester.*
**After:** *No courses yet. What would you like to take?*

### Error (connection lost while saving)

**Before:** *The magic fizzled. Try again?*
**After:** *We couldn't save your changes. Check your connection and try again.*

When data is at risk, the voice steps aside.

### Toast on first plan saved

**Before:** *Plan saved successfully.*
**After:** *Wish locked in.*

First-time moment, low stakes, high delight. Earns the brand beat.

## Rationale

This voice framework resolves three tensions in the product.

**1. Two audiences, one surface.**
Students want delight; parents want trust. Whimsy that charms a 15-year-old can unsettle a 45-year-old paying $9.99/month. The framework resolves this by reserving wish-voice for moments students see first (empty states, onboarding, microcopy) and keeping commit surfaces (CTAs, pricing, billing) neutral and credible.

**2. The Aladdin trap.**
[`brand.md`](./brand.md) documents how much design work went into *avoiding* Disney/Aladdin trade-dress — green skin, no lamp, no incantation vocabulary. The copy has to respect the same guardrails. If the page says *"your wish is my command,"* the green skin isn't saving us.

**3. Clarity compounds.**
A landing page has two seconds to explain itself. A dashboard has 200ms per element. Every word of theming is a word the reader pays for in comprehension. Voice has to earn its place against clarity — and usually loses on the surfaces where users are deciding.

## Maintenance

When new surfaces get added (new pages, new email templates, new error types), update the **Surface-by-surface guide** table rather than ad-libbing the voice register per location. Drift happens when contributors make per-surface judgment calls without a shared frame.

When you find copy that's wrong, fix it — and if the fix illustrates a principle not yet captured, add it to the **Examples** section.

Voice is a product of iteration, not a single pass. Expect to revisit this doc after the first real A/B test on the hero.

### Enforcement

User-visible copy in `.tsx` files, public markdown, and email templates is reviewed automatically on every PR by **voice-guardian** ([`.github/workflows/voice-guardian.yml`](../../.github/workflows/voice-guardian.yml)). It loads this doc and [`brand.md`](./brand.md) on every run, evaluates changed strings against the rubric, and posts findings as a non-blocking PR review with severity labels (`error` / `warn` / `info`). The skill rubric lives at [`.claude/skills/voice-guardian/SKILL.md`](../../.claude/skills/voice-guardian/SKILL.md); invoke it as `/voice-guardian` in Claude Code to dry-run against the working diff before pushing.

Voice-guardian does not block merges. It is a second pair of eyes, not a gate. When the rubric is wrong, fix the rubric — don't ignore the finding.
